Which home changes require planning permission (and how to get it)

A clear, practical guide to what you can do to your home under permitted development — and when you'll need to apply to the council first.

SW
Sarah Whitmore Head of Sales
3rd June 2026  ·  5 min read
[ article hero image — 16:7 ]

Few things cause more confusion for homeowners than planning permission. The reassuring news is that a surprising amount of work can be done under what's called permitted development — with no application at all. Here's how to tell the difference, and what to do when consent is genuinely needed.

Permitted development: what you can usually do

Permitted development rights let you make certain changes to your home without a full planning application. They exist to save homeowners — and councils — time on uncontroversial projects. Common examples include:

  • Single-storey rear extensions within set depth, height and boundary limits
  • Loft conversions and rear dormers within volume allowances (40m³ for terraces, 50m³ for detached and semi-detached homes)
  • Replacing windows and doors, and most internal alterations
  • Outbuildings, garden offices and conservatories within size limits
  • Solar panels, provided they sit within agreed projection limits
Permitted development isn't a blank cheque — every right comes with conditions on size, height and how close you can build to a boundary.

Those conditions matter. Exceed them by even a small margin and the work tips over into requiring a full application. And if your home is a flat, a listed building, or sits in a conservation area, National Park or Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty, many permitted development rights are restricted or removed altogether.

When you'll almost always need permission

Some projects fall outside permitted development by their very nature. Plan to apply if your project involves:

  • A new dwelling, annexe or anything creating a separate home
  • Extensions beyond the permitted size, height or coverage limits
  • Changing the use of a building — for example, a shop to a home
  • Most work to a listed building, inside or out
  • Anything affecting a protected tree or a shared boundary
The quick version
  • Check the permitted development limits before assuming you need consent
  • Conservation areas and listed buildings have far tighter rules
  • A Lawful Development Certificate is cheap insurance that work is legal
  • Apply early — householder decisions take around eight weeks

How to check and apply

If you're unsure, the safest first step is a Lawful Development Certificate. It's a formal confirmation from your council that your project is lawful and doesn't need permission — invaluable when you come to sell. Where a full application is needed, the typical route is:

  1. Confirm your site's constraints with your local planning authority
  2. Prepare drawings and a short design statement
  3. Submit through the Planning Portal and pay the fee
  4. Await a decision — usually around eight weeks for householder applications

Getting this right protects your investment and keeps a future sale running smoothly. If you're weighing up an extension or conversion and want to understand how it might affect your home's value, our team is always happy to talk it through.
